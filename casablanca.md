# They had a date with fate in Casablanca

One of those films you expect to admire more than feel. It has been praised to death, canonized, polished into something distant. But watching it again, it’s insane how alive it still is.

It understands that conviction and sacrifice are rarely clean or romantic. The film is openly anti fascist, but what gives it weight is how tired everyone feels carrying that responsibility. Love is present, but it is never allowed to win outright. Hope exists, but only because people choose it while knowing what it costs. Released at a moment when the outcome of the war was far from certain, the film feels less like propaganda and more like reassurance that choosing decency still matters.

Technically, it is beautiful. The lighting gives faces room to hide and reveal themselves. The dialogue moves with confidence. Every arc resolves without strain. The final airport scene, with Rick Blaine and Captain Louis Renault - utter perfection!!!
